Opening a restaurant without a disciplined equipment plan is one of the fastest ways to burn through capital before you serve plate one. Commercial kitchen equipment is not a shopping list, it is the physical expression of your concept, labor model, and health department compliance. This commercial kitchen equipment buying guide walks new owners through budgeting, specification, vendor selection, and installation sequencing so you buy once, buy correctly, and stay inside your [prime cost](/glossary/prime-cost) targets from day one.

Whether you are building a scratch kitchen, a fast-casual assembly line, or a ghost brand from a [commissary](/glossary/commissary), the same rule applies: equipment decisions made in month one echo through your [food cost percentage](/glossary/food-cost-percentage) and [labor cost percentage](/glossary/labor-cost-percentage) for years. Pair this guide with our [restaurant equipment checklist for new owners](/blog/restaurant-equipment-checklist-new-owners) and your [restaurant concept development](/blog/restaurant-concept-development-guide) work so every purchase supports the guest experience you promised.

## Why Does Equipment Planning Start Before You Sign a Lease?

Equipment is not portable in the way many first-time operators imagine. A 10-foot [hood system](/glossary/hood-system) requires ceiling height, makeup air, and fire suppression that your landlord may not approve. A [combi oven](/glossary/combi-oven) needs dedicated water filtration, drain, and electrical capacity. Walk-in coolers dictate floor plans and door swings. That is why experienced operators run equipment programming in parallel with [how to choose a restaurant location](/blog/how-to-choose-restaurant-location). The wrong box size or utility package can make your dream menu physically impossible.

Before you commit capital, answer four site questions with your architect, mechanical engineer, and equipment dealer:

1. **Gas or electric?** Gas cooking is standard for high-BTU line cooking; electric may be required in some urban cores or when landlords restrict gas extensions.
2. **Electrical service size?** Add nameplate amps for refrigeration, dish machines, combi ovens, and HVAC, then add 20% growth headroom.
3. **Hood and exhaust path?** Type I vs Type II, duct routing, roof penetrations, and neighbor restrictions can add $30,000–$120,000 to a build.
4. **Delivery access?** Freight elevators, curb cuts, and door widths determine whether you can install a 40" range or must spec modular pieces.

## How Should You Build a Menu-Driven Equipment List?

Start with a station map, not a catalog. For each menu category, document volume (covers per hour at peak), cooking method, hold time, and allergen/cross-contact needs. Your [restaurant market analysis](/blog/restaurant-market-analysis-guide) should already tell you what dayparts matter; equipment must win those dayparts without overbuilding slow periods.

| Station | Typical core equipment | Spec drivers |
|---------|------------------------|--------------|
| Line cooking | Range, flat top, fryer, [salamander](/glossary/salamander) | Peak tickets/hour, menu mix, fuel type |
| Finishing | Heat lamps, pass, plating counters | Pickup model (dine-in vs delivery) |
| Prep | Mixer, slicer, prep tables, refrigeration | Batch sizes, scratch vs prepped |
| Pastry / brunch | Convection, [combi oven](/glossary/combi-oven), proofer | Daypart concentration |
| Cold kitchen | Walk-in, reach-ins, [blast chiller](/glossary/blast-chiller) | Inventory turns, HACCP plan |
| Warewash | Door-type or undercounter dish machine | Peak glass/rack volume, water hardness |

Cross-reference this table with the detailed line items in our [equipment checklist](/blog/restaurant-equipment-checklist-new-owners). Remove anything that does not touch a recipe step. "Nice to have" fryers are expensive paperweights.

### What is the difference between production and finishing equipment?

Production equipment (ranges, fryers, steamers) sets your maximum throughput. Finishing equipment (salamanders, heat lamps, sauciers at the pass) sets perceived quality and ticket times. Under-invest in production and you cap revenue; under-invest in finishing and you get cold fries and one-star reviews. For delivery-heavy concepts, also budget holding cabinets and bagging stations, they are part of the guest experience even if guests never see your kitchen.

## How Much Should You Budget for Commercial Kitchen Equipment in 2026?

Budget ranges vary by concept, but planning bands help you sanity-check quotes against your [opening cost model](/blog/how-much-does-it-cost-to-open-a-restaurant-2026):

| Concept type | Equipment + smallwares (% of startup) | Illustrative equipment band (USD) |
|--------------|----------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Coffee / bakery | 25–35% | $80,000 – $250,000 |
| Fast casual | 20–30% | $150,000 – $400,000 |
| Full service | 15–25% | $250,000 – $600,000+ |
| Bar-forward | 15–20% | $200,000 – $500,000 |
| Ghost / delivery kitchen | 30–40% | $100,000 – $300,000 |

Add **10–15% contingency** for freight, rigging, installation, fire suppression, and unexpected code upgrades. Smallwares, pots, pans, utensils, thermometers, often run **8–12% of equipment spend**; do not lump them into "miscellaneous."

Financing can preserve working capital for payroll and pre-opening marketing, but compare APR, term length, and personal guarantees. A lower monthly payment on a 60-month lease is not a win if you upgrade equipment in year four and face payoff penalties.

## New vs Used vs Refurbished: What Actually Makes Sense?

| Category | Buy new | Consider used/refurb |
|----------|---------|----------------------|
| Hoods & fire suppression | ✓ | Rarely, code and warranty risk |
| Refrigeration | ✓ | Only with verified compressor logs |
| Dish machines | ✓ | Refurb from authorized dealers only |
| Cooking lines | ✓ preferred | Used ranges/ovens with inspection |
| Prep tables, shelving | Either | ✓ strong used market |
| Smallwares | Either | ✓ |

When evaluating used pieces, verify model numbers against current NSF listings, confirm parts availability, and get a licensed technician's written assessment before purchase. Document gas connection sizes and electrical phases, mismatches turn bargains into change orders.

## How Do You Evaluate Vendors and Specifications Like a Pro?

Treat equipment quotes as technical documents, not price lists. For each major item, compare:

- **Dimensions and utilities** (gas BTU, electrical FLA, water GPM, drain size)
- **Production capacity** (pans per hour, recovery time after load)
- **Warranty** (parts, labor, on-site response windows)
- **Installation scope** (who pulls permits for gas, who commissions fire suppression)
- **Lead time and cancellation terms**

National suppliers like [Horeca Store](https://www.thehorecastore.com/restaurant-equipment) aggregate hundreds of brands with transparent specs, useful when you need to compare three combi models side by side without visiting five local dealers. Local dealers may bundle installation; national partners may win on price and SKU breadth. Many operators split the buy: critical path items (hood, walk-in, dish) through local installers; cooking and prep through specialized supply partners.

Ask every vendor:

1. Who owns the warranty relationship, the dealer or the manufacturer?
2. What is the mean time to repair for this model in your market?
3. Will you provide submittals for health and building departments?
4. What happens if the item arrives damaged or short-shipped during buildout?

## What Installation Sequencing Prevents Costly Delays?

Equipment installation is a critical path activity tied to MEP rough-in, hood installation, health plan review, and final inspection. A practical sequence:

1. **Design development**: Equipment schedule signed by chef and GM; submitted to architect.
2. **Rough-in**: Gas, water, drains, electrical stub-ups per approved submittals.
3. **Hood and exhaust**: Installed and balanced before cooking equipment is set.
4. **Refrigeration**: Walk-ins and large reach-ins early (long lead times).
5. **Cooking line**: Set, leveled, connected, and commissioned.
6. **Dish and ventless**: Often last; still before health pre-inspection.
7. **Startup & training**: Vendor training for combi, fryer filtration, and fire suppression.

Slip any step and you slip your opening date, which burns rent and staff onboarding budget. Align your equipment PO dates with lease delivery and [permit timelines](/blog/restaurant-permits-licenses-guide).

## How Does Equipment Choice Affect Long-Term Profitability?

Equipment efficiency shows up in utility bills, labor minutes, and waste. A [combi oven](/glossary/combi-oven) can replace multiple pieces and reduce batch inconsistency, improving yield and lowering [food cost percentage](/glossary/food-cost-percentage). A [blast chiller](/glossary/blast-chiller) extends shelf life on proteins and sauces, reducing spoilage on high-cost SKUs. Conversely, oversized fryers and underused steamers inflate [prime cost](/glossary/prime-cost) through depreciation, cleaning chemicals, and training overhead.

Energy Star and idle-load features matter more as utilities rise. Model annual gas and electric at 70% and 100% capacity, if the delta breaks your pro forma, right-size the line.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much should a new restaurant spend on kitchen equipment?

Most full-service independents allocate 15–25% of total startup capital to kitchen equipment and smallwares, while fast-casual concepts often land near 20–30% because cooking lines are equipment-heavy. Always model equipment against your menu and projected covers, not a generic checklist.

### Should I buy new or used commercial kitchen equipment?

Buy new for items tied to health code, warranty, and throughput, refrigeration, hood systems, dish machines, and primary cooking lines. Used can work for prep tables, shelving, and some ovens if you verify service history, gas/electrical compatibility, and local code acceptance.

### What equipment requires a Type I hood?

Any appliance that produces grease-laden vapors, open broilers, charbroilers, woks, fryers, and most gas ranges, typically requires a Type I hood with automatic fire suppression. Steam-only or electric equipment may qualify for Type II; confirm with your mechanical engineer and authority having jurisdiction.

### How long does commercial kitchen equipment delivery take in 2026?

In-stock items often ship in 3–10 business days; custom or imported pieces (large combi ovens, walk-ins) can take 8–16 weeks. Order hoods and walk-ins as soon as lease and MEP drawings are approved to avoid delaying your health inspection.

### Can I finance restaurant equipment instead of paying cash?

Yes, equipment financing, leasing, and vendor programs are common. Compare total cost of ownership, early payoff penalties, and whether the lender files a UCC lien that affects future credit lines for buildout.
